# The MOS 6502 (1975): The 8-Bit Democratizer

**The Rule:** The "engine" that drove the home computer revolution. Because it was incredibly cheap to produce, it allowed computers and consoles to move from the lab to the living room, powering the Apple II, Commodore 64, and NES.

---

### **The Technical Pivot**

In 1975, the MOS 6502 was released at a price point ($\$25$) that was nearly a tenth of its competitors. This affordability allowed engineers like Steve Wozniak to build the Apple I and II, and enabled Commodore to create the C64—the best-selling computer model in history.

### **The "Why It Mattered" Shift**

* **The Library of the 80s:** Its popularity created a massive ecosystem of developers, resulting in the most diverse software library of the century.
* **The Gaming Foundation:** From the Atari 2600 to the NES, the 6502 architecture defined the first three generations of home gaming, establishing the logic for titles like *Super Mario Bros.* and *The Legend of Zelda*.

### **The "Street-Level" Perspective**

* **The Social Machine:** While the TI-99/4A was a technical powerhouse, the 6502 machines were the "social" hubs. The sheer volume of users meant that the 6502 defined the "community" aspect of early computing—from disk-swapping to local user groups.
* **The Arcade Reality:** The arrival of the NES powered by the 6502 made previous home versions of arcade games feel like relics. It was the moment home entertainment achieved "arcade parity," making the high-end movie-tie-in dreams of the 80s finally feel real.
